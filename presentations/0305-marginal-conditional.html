Steps in the Marginal-Conditional Method

  1. Pick which uncertainty you'll model as "marginal", and which you'll model as "conditional".
  2. Build the model of the marginal uncertainty.
  3. Build EACH model of the conditional uncertainty.

Steps in building a Marginal-Conditional Diagram

  1. Draw a rectangle.
  2. Divide the horizontal axis proportionally to depict the marginal uncertainty.
  3. Divide EACH horizontal segment proportionally along the vertical axis to depict the conditional uncertainty.

Try it

Try it

Imagine a chief executive who has the power to set policy to one of two locations -- $L$ and $R$, where $L \lt R$. Suppose the chief executive is a kleptocrat who does not care about the location of policy, but instead is only interested in looting the government to enrich himself and his family. Suppose also that he has a very short attention span.

Suppose the executive has two advisors who each care passionately about policy. Advisor $A_L$ prefers $L$ to $R$ and advisor $A_R$ prefers $R$ to $L$.

Since the executive does not care about policy and has a very short attention span, he will tolerate only one conversation about policy with only one advisor per day, lasting about 5 minutes.

Whichever advisor is lucky enough to get that 5 minutes will try very hard to persuade the chief executive to implement her more-preferred of the two policies. But the chief executive's limited attention span makes it impossible to be sure which policy he will implement once the conversation ends.

Using a marginal-conditional diagram, build a model that depicts uncertainty about two things:

  • Which advisor will get the one 5-minute conversation with the chief executive.
  • Which policy the chief executive will implement.

Depict the idea that the likelihood that the chief executive will implement policy $R$ is higher if advisor $A_R$ gets to have the one five-minute conversation with the executive than if advisor $A_L$ gets to have the one five-minute conversation with the executive.
