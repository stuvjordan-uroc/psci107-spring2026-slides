Where this fits

Remember...
A PPT model of a person's preferences consists of a set of alternatives and a binary comparison for each unique pair of alternatives.
The Spatial Model...
Is a way of modeling a set of alternatives (over which a person might have preferences), when those alternatives are policies that the government could adopt toward a given issue.
Example: Proactive vs Reactive Policing
Example: Proactive vs. Reactive Policing
Reactive operations...
...are initiated by civilians or other persons outside of the police force. They amount to the police waiting for 911 calls, responding to them, and making arrests and/or initiating investigations when response to a 911 call reveals a crime.
Proactive operations...
...are initiated by police without external requests. They are meant to disrupt criminal activity as or before it happens. Examples include speed traps, undercover drug/gun stings, surveillance operations aimed at criminal enterprises, and community outreach.
Example: Proactive vs. Reactive Policing
City police forces provide a mix of proactive and reactive police services, and city governments make choices about how much of each type of policing to provide.
For example, a city government can choose...
  • Which portion of each patrol shift to assign to wait for and respond to 911 calls, (assigning the remaining portion to proactive details, such as traffic enforcement).
  • How many officers to assign to vice, organized crime, and trafficking units, which run exclusively pro-active operations.
  • How many officers to assign to homicide and/or general investigative units, which run (almost) exclusively reactive operations.
Example: Proactive vs. Reactive Policing
Notice: Any given choice about the allocation of police resources across different types of operations has a degree of proactive-ness.
This means that we can order different possible policies relative to one another according to their degree of proactive-ness.
For example:
What percentages of officers on a patrol shift should be assigned to wait-for-and-respond-to 911 calls (with the remainder assigned to pro-active details)?
This is a spatial model of choices over how to allocate police resources on a patrol shift.

Example
Description of a policy issue
How should written laws enacted by a legislature be enforced? There are all kinds of choices that have to be made about this. For instance: How are violations of the law detected? Who decides whether an alleged violation will be punished and how do they decide? What punishments are applied to those found culpable of violations?
Description of an ordering criterion
One feature that differentiates enforcement systems from one another is the extent to which they rely on public (as opposed to private) enforcement. An enforcement system is public to the extent that only employees of the states (such as prosecutors and police) are empowered to investigate and charge persons with violating a law.
Illustration with a few specific policies
In the U.S. most criminal laws rely on purely public enforcement. At the other extreme, enforcement of personal injury law tends to be purely private, with only private inviduals empowered to bring suit against a person alleged to have harmed them in violation of law. U.S. laws against fraud in federal procurement use a mixed system, with both private individuals and U.S. attoneys empowered to bring charges against individuals for fraud in federal contracts.
Example
Description of a policy issue
What regulations should apply to banks that provide saving and checking accounts, loans, and investment instruments to individuals (as opposed to corporations)?
Description of an ordering criterion
One feature that differentiates regulatory regimes for consumer banks are strictness of reserve requirements. Strict reserve regimes require consumer banks to hold large portions of their customer's assets as cash. Less strict reserve requirements allow banks to use more of customer deposits for making loans and investments.
