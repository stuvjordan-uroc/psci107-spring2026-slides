Where this fits

Remember...
A PPT model of a person's preferences consists of a set of alternatives and a binary comparison for each unique pair of alternatives.
The Spatial Model...
Is a way of modeling a set of alternatives (over which a person might have preferences), when those alternatives are policies that the government could adopt toward a given issue.

Example: Proactive vs. Reactive Policing
Reactive operations...
...are initiated by civilians or other persons outside of the police force. They amount to the police waiting for 911 calls, responding to them, and making arrests and/or initiating investigations when response to a 911 call reveals a crime.
Proactive operations...
...are initiated by police without external requests. They are meant to disrupt criminal activity as or before it happens. Examples include speed traps, undercover drug/gun stings, surveillance operations aimed at criminal enterprises, and community outreach.
Example: Proactive vs. Reactive Policing
City police forces provide a mix of proactive and reactive police services, and city governments make choices about how much of each type of policing to provide.
For example, a city government can choose...
  • Which portion of each patrol shift to assign to wait for and respond to 911 calls, (assigning the remaining portion to proactive details, such as traffic enforcement).
  • How many officers to assign to vice, organized crime, and trafficking units, which run exclusively pro-active operations.
  • How many officers to assign to homicide and/or general investigative units, which run (almost) exclusively reactive operations.

Notice: Any given choice about the allocation of police resources across different types of operations has a degree of proactive-ness.
This means that we can order different possible policies relative to one another according to their degree of proactive-ness.
