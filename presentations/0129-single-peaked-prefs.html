Where single peaked preferences fit

You've learned the general model of a persons preferences
...a set alternatives and one binary ordering for each unique pair of alternatives.
You've learned the spatial model of policy
...a way of modeling a set alternatives that are policies that could be adopted by a government.
Now you'll learn single peaked preferences
...a simple way to model preferences when the alterantives are policies depicted by a spatial model.

Defining single peaked preferences

Defining single peaked preferences
Take any spatial model of a set of policies.

A person's preferences over those policies are single peaked if:

  1. There is a location in the model call the person's ideal point.

  2. Given any pair of policies $x$ and $y$...

    • If $x$ is closer to her ideal point than $y$, she prefers $x$ to $y$.
    • If $y$ is closer to her ideal point than $x$, she prefers $y$ to $x$.
    • If $x$ and $y$ are equidistant from her ideal point, she is indifferent between $x$ and $y$.

Spatial voting and the midpoint rule

The Simplest Model of (Spatial) Voting

Take any set of persons who have single-peaked preferences in some spatial model

Take any pair of policies in the model

Imagine the persons take a vote between the policies, with each person casting her vote for the policy she finds more preferable.

The Midpoint Rule

Take the midpoint between any two policies in a spatial model.

  • Every person with single-peaked preferences who has an ideal point to the left of the midpoint prefers the left policy to the right policy.
  • Every person with single-peaked preferences who has an ideal point to the right of the midpoint prefers the right policy to the left policy.
We will always put policies on the top and persons' ideal points on the bottom!
  • Persons K, L, M, and N prefer X to Y (and thus vote for X).
  • Persons O, P, Q, R, and S prefer Y to X (and thus vote for Y).
