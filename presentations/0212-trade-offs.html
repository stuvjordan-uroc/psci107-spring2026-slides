The Actual Survey
  • About 20,000 respondents (all likely voters)
  • Asks for positions on 4 different issues -- Immigration, marijuana legalization, school funding, income tax rates.
  • Presented with 16 different hypothetical candidate pairs.
  • Policy position contrast in each pair randomly chosen from the four issues.
  • Candidate aligned with voter on policy takes anti-democratic position chosen at random from a list.
  • Candidate mis-aligned with voter on policy takes some "neutral" position on something uninteresting, randomly chosen from a list.

Anti-Democratic Positions

  • Supported proposal to reduce the number of polling stations in areas that support [opposite party].
  • Said the [own party] governor should prosecute journalist who accuse him of misconudct without revealing sources.
  • Said the [own party] governor should rule by executive order if [opposite party] legislators don't cooperate
  • Said the [own party] governor should ban far-[opposite side of political spectrum] group rallies in the state capital.
  • Supported redistricting plan that gives [own party] 2 extra seats despite a decline in the polls.

Neutral/Uninteresting Positions

  • Served on a committee that establishes the state legislature's schedule for each session.
  • Worked on a plan to change the state legislature's committee structure
  • Served on the state's Board of Elections, which handles local, state, and federal elections.
  • Submitted a proposal that would change the state's record-keeping laws and practices.
  • Served on a subcommittee that reviews the structure of state legislative staff offices.

Modeling the tradeoff

Model
Imagine a voter choosing which of two candidates -- $L$ and $R$ -- to vote for.
The candidates have taken positions $x_L$ and $x_R$ on a policy issue.
The voter knows that if candidate $L$ wins, she will attack democratic institutions, and if candidate $R$ wins he will not attack democratic institutions.
The voter's utility from voting for a candidate with policy position $x$ is: $$ - | x - p | + \begin{cases} c & \text{if the candidate will attack democratic institutions} \\ 0 & \text{if the candidate will not attack democratic institutions} \end{cases} $$
Assume $x_L \lt p \lt x_R$, and $c$ is just some number (the voter might like attacks on democracy!)

Analyzing the Model

Typical series of exam prompts!
  1. Write the expression for the voter's utility from voting for candidate $L$.
  2. Write the expression for the voter's utility from voting for candidate $R$.
  3. Write the inequality that describes the conditions under which the voter prefers voting for candidate $L$ to voting for candidate $R$.
  4. Rearrange the inequality from the previous step so that $x_R$ is by itself on one side of the inequality. (Remember, $x_L \lt p \lt x_R$).
  5. Draw a diagram that shows the threshold on $x_R$ that determines whether the voter prefers voting for $L$ to voting for $R$.
  6. Describe how the value of $c$ affects the willingness of a voter to vote for the anti-democratic candidate $L$, given $p$, $x_L$, and $x_R$.

Modeling Challenge

Make it better
This utility function is kind of lame: $$ - | x - p | + \begin{cases} c & \text{if the candidate will attack democratic institutions} \\ 0 & \text{if the candidate will not attack democratic institutions} \end{cases} $$
It depicts the effect of a candidate being anti-democratic as independent of the policy position of the candidate.
Make up a new utility function that depicts a candidate's anti-democratic-ness making the voter's utility from voting for the candidate...
  • ...higher to the extent that the candidate's policy position is close to the voter's ideal point.
  • ...lower to the extent that the candidate's policy position is far from the voter's ideal point.
